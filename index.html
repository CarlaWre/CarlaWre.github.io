I. Introduction

Recent developments in ICT technology enable more and more data to be collected. According to Forbes [1], already back in 2018 around 90% of the world’s data was generated in the span of only two years. The sources of this are varying, with individuals creating parts of this data more as a byproduct of social media usage. Contrastingly in industries, the data collection process is purposefully accelerated, especially with the rise of Internet of Things (IoT) technologies. Companies and organizations see a potential in these large amounts of data to be used to support decision-making processes. For this, more data mining and knowledge discovery methods are deployed in companies. The level of insight from this varies per company but can be classified into one of four categories that have been proposed by Gartner to distinguish the level of analytics: descriptive analytics, diagnostic analytics, predictive analytics and prescriptive analytics [2].

Figure 1:
Gartner analytics model. Taken from https://computd.nl/demystification/4-levels-of-data-maturity/, cite this correctly[?]

The basic level, requiring the least data maturity is descriptive analytics. The aim is to provide a description of current or past processes and give an elementary understanding of the data, so that the user can make the best decision themselves. This data description can include statistics (numbers), visualizations (e.g., plots) and verbalizations (e.g., linguistic summaries). The next level of diagnostic analytics then tries to find a reason for the situation as ascertained in the previous level. Predictive analytics deals with using the accumulated historical data to make predictions for the future. Finally, the last level of prescriptive analytics aims to use the predictions from the previous models and apply actions such that a desired outcome can be achieved.

While there are many possible ways to describe data or results, this paper focuses on linguistic summaries. Linguistic summaries are template-based sentences in (semi-)natural language that capture the essence of the data. In the following, we explain the concept of linguistic summaries, their building blocks and how they can be evaluated automatically. Moreover, they can not only be used for the descriptive analytics, but also for the later stages in the analytics model, as showcased in the application section.

III. Linguistic Variable

The key concepts of the linguistic summaries and fuzzy set theory are the linguistic variable and the linguistic value. Formally, according to Klir and Yuan, a linguistic variable is defined as a quintuple (v,T,X,g,m), where v is the name of the variable, T is the set of linguistic terms of v, X is the universal set representing the range of v, g is a syntactic rule for generating linguistic terms, and m is a semantic rule that assigns to each linguistic term $$ t∈T $$ its meaning, m(t), which is a fuzzy set on X. Let us consider the iris data set and the linguistic variable sepal length. In this case, linguistic terms may be small, medium and large. X is a set [4.3, 7.9], as this is the range of the sepal length. Each of the linguistic terms is mapped to a fuzzy set. Figure 4 illustrates the structure of the linguistic variable sepal length.

IV. Linguistic quantifier

A special kind of linguistic variable, essential in linguistic summaries is a (linguistic) quantifier. Linguistic quantifier describe a quantity in linguistic terms. Quantifiers are divided into two main classes: absolute quantifiers and relative quantifiers. Absolute quantifiers are defined over numbers, $$\R$$, and represent absolute quantities, such as “around 5”, “at least 10”. The relative quantifiers, exemplified by “at least a half”, “most”, are defined on interval [0,1]. Figure 6 shows examples of both absolute quantifiers (a) and relative quantifiers (b).

V. Linguistic Summary

What is a linguistic summary?

The notion of linguistic summary was introduced by R.R. Yager [Yag82, Yag91] as a fuzzy logic-based approach to the summarization of data. It is very frequently used as a synonym for linguistic description (or as part of it) in the NLG community, see for instance [SRHY03, MS16].

Yager’s approach consider linguistic summaries described by means of quantified statements of the form Q [R] y’s are P, where:

  • Q is a linguistic quantifier (e.g. most).
  • Y’s are the objects (e.g. tuples in a database) over which we want to provide the summary.
  • P is called the summarizer, and corresponds to a fuzzy subset of Y. It is usual that P is induced by some linguistic label defined on the domain of one variable defining some aspect of objects in Y. For instance, if we consider Y to be a set of people and the variable height, and we consider the linguistic label tall defined on the domain of possible heights of people, then P can be defined as the set of tall people, the membership of each person y to P being the membership degree of the height of y to the label tall.
  • R is an optional argument called qualifier, corresponding to a fuzzy subset of Y. It allows to restrict the summary to those objects in Y that satisfy R. As with P, it can be induced by linguistic labels on the domain of variables of objects. Note that, in practice, avoiding the use of a qualifier is equivalent to consider that R=Y.

As an example we can consider the quantified statements Most of the people are tall, where Q is the quantifier most, Y is a set of people (the specific set for which we want to perform the summary), and tall stands for the fuzzy subset of tall people defined on Y. In this case it is R=Y or, if you prefer it, we are avoiding the use of a qualifier R. Another example is Most of the young people are tall, where in this case we have used as qualifier the fuzzy subset of young people within Y.

On the basis of these quantified statements, a linguistic summary is defined as a pair comprised of:

  • A quantified statement of the form Q [R] y’s are P, and
  • A truth value T, corresponding to the accomplishment degree of the quantified statement on the dataset. It represents to which degree the quantified statement is accurate for the data being summarized.

Let’s see how to compute T in the next section.

Computing the truth value

The computation of T for a given quantified statement defined by Q, Y, R, and P, has been the object of intense research in the fuzzy set community, see [DRSV14] for a review. Two main cases can be considered:

  • When Q is an absolute quantifier, it refers to the cardinality of the fuzzy set P. Hence, the way to use the qualifier R (when it appears) for restricting the sentence to the objects satisfying R is to combine R and P using a t-norm, obtaining this way the fuzzy set to which Q refers to. In this case, Q [R] y’s are P becomes Q y’s are R ∧ P. For instance, Around 3 young people are tall becomes Around 3 people are young and tall.
  • When Q is a relative quantifier, it refers to the so-called relative cardinality of the fuzzy set P with respect to the fuzzy set R, which is computed by dividing the cardinality of R ∧ P by the cardinality of R (which requires that R is never empty!). Note that when R is not employed we are in the case R=Y and we compute the relative cardinality by dividing the cardinality of P=Y ∧ P by the cardinality of Y.

Cardinality is computed on the basis of the classical sigma-count (sum of membership degrees).

Though easy to implement and interpret, and efficient (being $$O(n)$$), this method does not satisfy several theoretical properties that are intuitively expected, as shown for instance in [DSM00, DRSV14]. Among the several alternatives available in the literature, we use here method GD, introduced in [DSM00] and which has been also discussed as a suitable method outside fuzzy set theory (Representations by Levels, Gradual Sets) in [RSD18]. Let us consider again $$Y = {y1, . . . , yn}$$ and $$∧ = min$$ as t-norm. The method works as follows:

  • For absolute quantifiers, let $$Λ = {α1, . . . , αk}$$ be the set of different membership values of $$R ∧ P$$ in Y that are greater than 0, plus the value 1 if $$R ∧ P$$ is not a normal set. Let $$α_{k+1} = \element Λ$$ by convention, and let $$α_i > α_{i+1}$$ for every 1 ≤ $$i$$ ≤ $$k$$. Then formula where $$(R ∧ P)_{αi}$$ is the alpha-cut of level αi of the fuzzy set (R ∧ P). Note that this method computes the matching between the quantifier and the crisp cardinality in each significant alpha-cut of the set $$R ∧ P$$, defined by Λ. These are then combined into a final degree by a weighted sum in which the weight is computed as the difference between the value alpha employed and the immediately lesser one in Λ.
  • For relative quantifiers, working by alpha-cuts requires that R is a normal set so that we avoid divisions by 0. When it is not, we can normalize it and apply the same scaling factor to the set R ∧ P, which guarantees that the relative cardinalities in each alpha-cut do not vary. Let r = max1≤j≤n R(yj ). Since R ̸= ∅ it is r > 0. Let R∗ be the normalization of R obtained by dividing each membership degree by r, and let RP∗ be the scaling of R ∧ P obtained by dividing each membership degree by r as well. Now, the set of significant levels Λ is obtained as the set of membership values to either R∗ or RP∗ (note that, as R∗ is a normal set, the value 1 is already in Λ). Hence we have again Λ = {α1, . . . , alphak} with αk+1 = 0 ̸∈ Λ by convention, and αi > αi+1 for every 1 ≤ i ≤ k. VI. Applications

Linguistic summaries have been applied to different type of data:

  • databases (cf. Kacprzyk et al.~\cite{KacprzykZadrozny2005A,KacprzykYagerZadrozny2001}) [?],
  • time series (Kacprzyk et al.~\cite{KacprzykWilbikZadrozny2008FuzzySets}, Castillo-Ortega et al.~\cite{Castillo-OrtegaMarinSanchez2011a}) [?],
  • standardized texts (cf. Szczepaniak~\cite{SzczepaniakOchelska2006}) [?],
  • videos (cf. Anderson et al.~\cite{AndersonLukeKellerSkubicRantzAud2009,AndersonLukeKellerSkubicRantzAud2009A,AndersonLukeKeller2010}) [?],
  • sensor data~(cf. Ros et al.~\cite{RosAndersonKellerPegalajarDelgadoVilaPopescu2011}, Wilbik et al.~\cite{WilbikKellerAlexander2011,WilbikKellerBezdek2013}) [?],
  • web logs (cf. Zadro\.zny and Kacprzyk~\cite{ZadroznyKacprzyk2007}) [?] and
  • event logs (cf. Wilbik~\cite{WilbikKaymak2014,WilbikDijkman2015,WilbikKaymak2015}) [?].

Moreover, linguistic summaries have found several real-world applications.

Strykowski et al. [?] described a use case in commerce, where linguistic summaries were used to identify and communicate the patterns in selling computer parts and weather in a B2C setting. Based on those patterns, the shop was predicting their sales and required personnel.

Linguistic summaries are also used to monitor the status of elderly residents in an eldercare facility, TigerPlace in Columbia, Missouri, USA [?]. In this project, motion sensors were placed in the apartments of the elderly residents. The sensors measure the activity level of their residents, that later is turned into linguistic description of activity of the residents. The linguistic summaries were proven to have sufficient sensitivity to capture the change in behavior or adverse health event [?].

Linguistic summaries are also used in the agri-food industry, to monitor the climate inside a pigsty [?]. The evaluations with the farmers, proved that linguistic summaries can be easily interpreted and understood by non-technically trained people.

Last, but not least, linguistic summaries seem to be good mechanisms for explanations. Initial works showed that the summaries can be used to find an explanation of an anomaly in the process data [?]. Moreover, linguistic summaries score highly in users' evaluations in terms of explanation understandability and usefulness [?].
