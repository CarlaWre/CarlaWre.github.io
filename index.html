I. Introduction

Recent developments in ICT technology enable more and more data to be collected. According to Forbes [1], already back in 2018 around 90% of the world’s data was generated in the span of only two years. The sources of data are varying, with individuals creating parts of this data more as a byproduct of social media usage. On the other side, companies, governmental, and non-governmental institutions accelerate the data collection process purposefully, especially with the rise of Internet of Things (IoT). Companies and organizations see a potential in these large amounts of data to be used to support decision-making processes. Because of the rise of both data and potential, more and more data mining and knowledge discovery methods are deployed in companies. The level of insight from this deployment varies per company but can be classified into one of four categories to distinguish the level of analytics, which can be see in Figure 1: descriptive analytics, diagnostic analytics, predictive analytics and prescriptive analytics.

Figure 1:
Business analytics maturity model (Picture from [2])

The basic level, requiring the least data maturity, is descriptive analytics. The aim is to provide a description of current or past processes and give an elementary understanding of the data, so that the user can make the best decision themselves. This data description can include statistics (numbers), visualizations (e.g., plots) and verbalizations (e.g., linguistic summaries). The next level of diagnostic analytics then tries to find a reason for the situation as ascertained in the previous level. Predictive analytics deals with using the accumulated historical data to make predictions for the future. Finally, the last level of prescriptive analytics aims to use the predictions from the previous models and apply actions such that a desired outcome can be achieved.

While there are many possible ways to describe data or results, this paper focuses on linguistic summaries. Linguistic summaries are template-based sentences in (semi-)natural language that capture the essence of the data. In the following, we explain the concept of linguistic summaries, their building blocks and how they can be evaluated automatically. Moreover, they can not only be used for the descriptive analytics, but also for the later stages in the analytics model, as showcased in the application section.

III. Background Fuzzy Sets

Before we elaborate on the topic of linguistic summaries, we will provide some background on sets. In traditional crisp sets, an element $x$ either belong to a set $A$, or it does not, as denoted by $x \in A$ or $x \notin A$. Crisp sets make use of bi-valued function logic, meaning that a set is a collection of definite, well-definable elements that form a whole. Their characteristic function $f_A$ can be denoted as follows: $f_A: X \rightarrow \{0,1\}$

However, real-world scenarios can most often not be denoted by simple binary states. Take for example a weather forecast of 18 degree Celsius; would you consider this a warm or a cold day? If there are clouds, one could consider it to be a fresher day, but if it is winter it might be a warm day. Here, the concept of fuzzy sets as introduced by Zadeh [5] comes into play. Fuzzy sets are related to vagueness and make use of infinite valued function logic. An element $x$ can belong to a fuzzy set $A$ to a certain amount, which is denoted by a degree of membership $\mu_A: X \rightarrow [0,1]$. This membership function (later on also denoted as $MF(X)$), can be defined in multiple ways, for example as a triangular function as seen in Figure 4 (right). A fuzzy set $A$ is completely determined by the set of ordered pairs $A=\{(x,\mu_A(x))| x \in X\}$, where $X$ is called the domain or universe of discourse. A visual comparison between the two types of sets can be seen in Figure 5.

Figure 4:
Example of crisp set (left) and fuzzy set (right).

Figure 5:
Comparison of crisp (top) and fuzzy sets (bottom) for the iris data set. (Figure adapted from [6].)

Just like with crisp sets, various operations can be applied on fuzzy sets. Fuzzy operations can be the same as for crisp sets when it comes to union, intersection and complement operations, on the examples of $(A \cup B) = max(A, B)$, $(A \cap B) = min(A, B)$, and $\neg A = 1 - A$, respectively. An example of these operations can be seen in Figure 6. These operations are called the standard fuzzy operations and are one way of generalizing the corresponding classical crisp operations, an example can be seen in Figure 6. However, there are many other ways on creating these generalizations, and such functions for unions and intersections are called t-norms and t-conorms, respectively. For more background information on fuzzy sets and their operations we can recommend reading [6].

Figure 6:
Example of possible operations: union (left), intersection (middle) and negation of A (right).

IV. Linguistic Variable

Important key concepts of linguistic summaries are linguistic variables and linguistic values. In the words of Zadeh [7],[8],[9], a linguistic variable is a variable whose values are words or sentences in a language (natural or artificial). Each of these values is called a term and is associated with a semantics that is nothing more than a fuzzy restriction defined on a base universal set.

According to [6],[7],[8],[9], a linguistic variable is defined as a quintuple $(v, T, X, g, m)$, where $v$ is the name of the variable, $T$ is the set of linguistic terms of $v$, $X$ is the universal set over which fuzzy restrictions are defined, $g$ is a syntactic rule for generating linguistic terms (a so called grammar), and $m$ is a semantic rule that assigns to each linguistic term its meaning, which is a fuzzy restriction on $X$.

In most cases simple grammars $g$ are used, in which the terms and their associated restrictions are provided directly. However, one could use a more complex, general grammar $g$, which allows to construct terms of the variable from base terms, connectives, and modifiers, for instance, generate very large from large, and quite small from small.

As an example, let us consider the iris data set and the linguistic variable sepal length. In this case, the set of linguistic terms may be $T={small, medium, large}$. Here, $X$ is the set [4.3, 7.9], as this is the range of the sepal length in the data set. Each linguistic term is mapped to a fuzzy set. V. Linguistic quantifier

A special kind of linguistic variable, essential in linguistic summaries is a (linguistic) quantifier. The linguistic quantifier describe a quantity in linguistic terms. Quantifiers are divided into two main classes: absolute quantifiers and relative quantifiers. Absolute quantifiers are defined over numbers, $$\mathbb{R}$$, and represent absolute quantities, such as “around 5”, “at least 10”. The relative quantifiers, exemplified by “at least a half”, “most”, are defined on interval [0,1]. Figure 9 shows examples of both absolute quantifiers (a) and relative quantifiers (b).

Figure 9:
Example of absolute quantifiers (left) and relative quantifiers (right).

VI. Linguistic Summary

What is a linguistic summary?

The notion of linguistic summary was introduced by Yager [10], [11] as a fuzzy logic-based approach to the summarization of data. It is very frequently used as a synonym for linguistic description (or as part of it) in the NLG community, see for instance [12], [13].

Yager’s approach consider linguistic summaries described by means of quantified statements of the form $Q$ [$R$] y’s are $P$, where:

  • $Q$ is a linguistic quantifier (e.g. most).
  • $Y$’s are the objects (e.g. tuples in a database) over which we want to provide the summary.
  • $P$ is called the summarizer, and corresponds to a fuzzy subset of $Y$. It is usual that $P$ is induced by some linguistic label defined on the domain of one variable defining some aspect of objects in $Y$. For instance, if we consider $Y$ to be a set of people and the variable height, and we consider the linguistic label tall defined on the domain of possible heights of people, then $P$ can be defined as the set of tall people, the membership of each person y to $P$ being the membership degree of the height of y to the label tall.
  • $R$ is an optional argument called qualifier, corresponding to a fuzzy subset of $Y$. It allows to restrict the summary to those objects in $Y$ that satisfy $R$. As with $P$, it can be induced by linguistic labels on the domain of variables of objects. Note that, in practice, avoiding the use of a qualifier is equivalent to consider that $R=Y$.

As an example we can consider the quantified statements Most of the people are tall, where $Q$ is the quantifier most, $Y$ is a set of people (the specific set for which we want to perform the summary), and tall stands for the fuzzy subset of tall people defined on $Y$. In this case it is $R=Y$ or, if you prefer it, we are avoiding the use of a qualifier $R$. Another example is Most of the young people are tall, where in this case we have used as qualifier the fuzzy subset of young people within $Y$.

On the basis of these quantified statements, a linguistic summary is defined as a pair comprised of:

  • A quantified statement of the form $Q$ [$R$] y’s are $P$, and
  • A truth value $T$, corresponding to the accomplishment degree of the quantified statement on the dataset. It represents to which degree the quantified statement is accurate for the data being summarized.

Let’s see how to compute $T$ in the next section.

How to calculate the truth value

The computation of $T$ for a given quantified statement defined by $Q$, $Y$, $R$, and $P$, has been the object of intense research in the fuzzy set community, see [14] for a review. Two main cases can be considered:

  • When $Q$ is an absolute quantifier, it refers to the cardinality of the fuzzy set $P$. Hence, the way to use the qualifier $R$ (when it appears) for restricting the sentence to the objects satisfying $R$ is to combine $R$ and $P$ using a t-norm, obtaining this way the fuzzy set to which $Q$ refers to. In this case, $Q$ $R$ y’s are $P$ becomes $Q$ y’s are $R ∧ P$. For instance, Around 3 young people are tall becomes Around 3 people are young and tall. Note that when R is not employed we have the case $R=Y$ and $Y ∧ P=P$.
  • When $Q$ is a relative quantifier, it refers to the so-called relative cardinality of the fuzzy set $P$ with respect to the fuzzy set $R$, which is computed by dividing the cardinality of $R ∧ P$ by the cardinality of $R$ (which requires that $R$ is never empty!). Note that when $R$ is not employed we are in the case $R=Y$ and we compute the relative cardinality by dividing the cardinality of $P=Y ∧ P$ by the cardinality of $Y$.

One of the most widely employed approaches, proposed by Zadeh [15] and employed in the original studies by Yager [10], [11], performs the computation as follows. Let us consider $$Y = {y_1, . . . , y_n}$$ and the t-norm minimum:

  • For absolute quantifiers:

    $$T_Z=Q \left( \sum_{i=1}^n \min (R(y_i),P(y_i)) \right)$$

    which, when $R=Y$, becomes

    $$T_Z=Q \left( \sum_{i=1}^n P(y_i) \right)$$

  • For relative quantifiers:

    $$T_Z=Q \left( \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n \min (R(y_i),P(y_i))}{\sum_{i=1}^n R(y_i)} \right)$$

    which, when $R=Y$, becomes

    $$T_Z=Q \left( \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n P(y_i)}{n} \right)$$

It is easy to see that this method computes $T$ in every case as the matching between the quantifier and the appropriate (absolute or relative) cardinality in each case, which is in accordance with the semantics of the quantified statement. Cardinality is computed on the basis of the classical sigma-count, which is the sum of membership degrees.

Before you define your linguistic summaries yourself in Figure 10, let us consider a toy example that illustrates how the truth value is calculated. Let $Y={y_1,y_2,y_3,y_4,y_5}$ be a set of five iris flowers, and $R= 1/y_1+0.7/y_2+0.6/y_3+0.4/y_4$ be the set of blue flowers and $P=0.95/y_1 +1/y_3+0.7/y_4 + 0.8/y_5$ be the pretty flowers. Let $Q$ be a relative quantifier most as shown in Figure 9. This quantifier is defined by equation:

$$ Q(x) = \begin{cases} 0 &\quad x \leq 0.5\\ 3\frac{1}{3}x + 1 \frac{2}{3} &\quad x \in (0.5, 0.8]\\ 1 &\quad x > 0.8\\ \end{cases} $$

Now, let us consider a simple from summary ($Q$ y’s are $P$), namely Most iris flowers are pretty. In order to calculate the truth value, we need to first calculate the number of pretty flowers. For this purpose, we calculate sigma-count of $P$, namely $Σ-count(P)=0.95+1+0.7+0.8=3.45$. Now, we calculate the proportion of pretty flowers in this set, as 3.45/5=0.69. As the last step, we calculate to what degree this proportion is most, i.e. we calculate $Q(0.69)=0.63$. We can indeed say that this sentence is relatively true.

Now, let us consider a summary in the extended form ($Q$ $R$y’s are $P$), Most blue iris flowers are pretty. In this case we first calculate which flowers are blue and pretty $P \cup R=0.95/y_1+0.6/y_3+0.4/y_4$, and next how many such flowers there are in the data set. The answer is $Σ-count(P \cup R)=0.95+0.6+0.4=1.9$. We also need to calculate how many flowers are blue. For this we also make use of sigma-counts, namely $Σ-count(R)=1+0.7+0.5+0.4=2.6$. Now, we can calculate the proportion of blue and pretty flowers against blue flowers in this set, as $1.95/2.6=0.75$. As the last step, we again calculate to what degree this proportion is most, i.e. we calculate $Q(0.75)=0.83$. We can indeed say that this sentence is (mostly) true.

Though easy to implement and interpret, and efficient (being $$O(n)$$), this method does not satisfy several theoretical properties that are intuitively expected, as shown for instance in [16]. There are several alternatives (cf. [14]) available in the literature, one of the most used methods is GD, which you can read up on in [16].

VII. Applications

Linguistic summaries have been adapted to summarize different types of data:

  • numeric data from databases (cf. Kacprzyk et al. [17], [18]),
  • time series (Kacprzyk et al. [19], Castillo-Ortega et al. [20]),
  • standardized texts (cf. Szczepaniak [21]),
  • description of images (cf. Castillo-Ortega [22]),
  • videos (cf. Anderson et al. [23],[24],[25]),
  • sensor data (cf. Ros et al. [26], Wilbik et al. [27], [28]),
  • web logs (cf. Zadrozny and Kacprzyk [29]) and
  • event logs (cf. Wilbik [30],[31],[32]).

Moreover, linguistic summaries have found several real-world applications.

Strykowski et al. [33] described a use case in commerce, where linguistic summaries were used to identify and communicate the patterns in selling computer parts and weather in a B2C setting. Based on those patterns, the shop was predicting their sales and required personnel.

Linguistic summaries are also used to monitor the status of elderly residents in an eldercare facility, TigerPlace in Columbia, Missouri, USA [34]. In this project, motion sensors were placed in the apartments of the elderly residents. The sensors measure the activity level of their residents, that later is turned into linguistic description of activity of the residents. The linguistic summaries were proven to have sufficient sensitivity to capture the change in behavior or adverse health events [35].

Linguistic summaries are also used in the agri-food industry, to monitor the climate inside a pigsty [36]. The evaluations with the farmers, proved that linguistic summaries can be easily interpreted and understood by non-technically trained people.

Last, but not least, linguistic summaries seem to be good mechanisms for explanations. Initial works showed that the summaries can be used to find an explanation of an anomaly in the process data [37]. Moreover, linguistic summaries score highly in users' evaluations in terms of explanation understandability and usefulness [38].

VIII. Conclusion

This paper introduced the concept of linguistic summaries as descriptive analytics tool. It is one technique for capturing data in form of natural language, which helps people with a non-technical background to understand the result from data analysis tools. The concept behind linguistic summaries, fuzzy sets, has been introduced, followed by the key concepts of linguistic variables and quantifiers. We have constructed a linguistic summary together and calculated its truth value and have seen some applications were linguistic summaries have been deployed. While this is just a brief introduction, there are many more aspects to linguistic summaries and we can recommend more interested readers a look into the cited references.
